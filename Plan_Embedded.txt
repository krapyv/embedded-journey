This is a very smart question to ask at the start of a long-term plan. The direct answer is yes, the landscape will shift, but the core fundamentals you are about to learn are your shield against obsolescence.

Here is what will change in the next two years, what will stay the same, and how your 24-month plan prepares you for both.

🧱 The Unshakeable Core: What Will NOT Change
The "basics" are not going anywhere. They are the physics of embedded systems. Your first 12-18 months are an investment in the skills that will be more valuable, not less, as systems get complex.

The C/C++ Foundation: Every single university module for 2026/27 still teaches C and C++ as the non-negotiable entry point for embedded systems . You cannot build a safe house on a weak foundation. New tools build on top of this, they don't replace it.

Core Peripherals (I2C, SPI, UART, CAN): These are the protocols that sensors, actuators, and ECUs use to talk to each other . The menus might change, but the dishes (I2C, SPI, etc.) will remain the same.

Real-Time Operating Systems (RTOS) & State Machines: As cars become centralized, managing multiple tasks with guaranteed timing is the core challenge . This is why you are learning FreeRTOS and state machines.

Debugging & Hardware Knowledge: The need to use logic analyzers, read datasheets, and understand a schematic will never be automated away. AI can write code, but it cannot probe a physical pin to see why it's not toggling .

🚀 The Major Shifts: What WILL Change (and Why You're Ready)
The industry is undergoing a massive transformation. By mastering the fundamentals, you position yourself to ride this wave, not be crushed by it.

1. The Rise of "Software-Defined Vehicles" (SDV)
The trend is moving from 100 small computers (ECUs) to a few powerful, centralized ones . This is where the complexity explodes. Synopsys predicts that by 2027, new cars will have over 600 million lines of code—double what they have today .

How your plan helps: Your plan directly targets this. The shift to centralization requires exactly the skills you are building: C++ for Adaptive AUTOSAR, RTOS for task management, and system-level thinking.

2. AI is Becoming a Development Tool (Not a Replacement)
New startups like H2LooP are building AI tools specifically to handle the grunt work of embedded engineering, like generating test cases or checking compliance with safety standards like ISO 26262 .

How your plan helps: You are learning the rules (MISRA, AUTOSAR) that these AI tools must follow. The engineer of the future won't be the one who resists AI, but the one who knows the domain well enough to supervise the AI's output and catch its mistakes.

3. The Virtualization of Hardware (Digital Twins)
The old way was to wait for the physical chip to arrive before coding. The new way is to use a "virtual development kit" (VDK) – a digital twin of the chip – to start coding 12 months earlier .

How your plan helps: This is a workflow change, not a fundamental skill change. You will still be writing C++ for an ARM core; you'll just be doing it on a laptop simulating the car, rather than on the physical hardware. Your deep knowledge of the hardware (Phase 3) will make you highly effective in a virtual environment.

🎯 How to Update Your Learning Plan for the Future
Your plan is already 90% there. Here are two small tweaks to future-proof it even more.

Double Down on C++ (You already are): The move to centralized, software-defined vehicles is a move toward complex C++ application layers. Your decision to add a dedicated C++ phase is the single most important thing you can do .

Look for "Virtual" Opportunities: In Months 21-24, after you've mastered the physical STM32, seek out introductions to Virtual Development Kits (VDKs). Understanding how to develop and test on a virtual ECU is a skill that will be in high demand by 2028 .

💡 The Bottom Line
You will not be "learning the wrong thing." By mastering C, C++, RTOS, and CAN, you are learning the vocabulary and grammar of this industry.

The industry will invent new dialects (new tools, new AI assistants), but you will be fluent enough to learn them in a week. The engineers who struggle will be those who skip the fundamentals and try to learn only the latest trendy framework.

Stay the course. Your plan is built for exactly this future.
